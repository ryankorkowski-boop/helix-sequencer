Snowman Band Concept Pack

Three distinct 8-9 foot snowman band lineups built around the same performer logic: a giant standup bass with four reactive strings, a six-string guitar with quick strum motion, a lead singer with exaggerated face animation, background vocal faces on the string players, and a drummer whose cymbals, kick, hi-hat, and snare can all ride stem-separated overlays.

Snowcap Swing

Warm brass palette Jazz club posture Crooner lead

This one leans elegant and classic, like the band is playing under old marquee lights on Christmas Eve.

  • The bassist reads best with isolated slap notes and deliberate left-hand climbs.
  • The guitarist feels like a hollow-body rhythm player with quick offbeat strums.
  • The singer has the clearest hero position if you want the face to dominate the yard.

Aurora Echo

Icy aurora palette Sharper geometry Modern staging

This is the sleek one. The silhouettes are still readable from the street, but the instruments feel crystalline and contemporary.

  • The four bass strings can glow in very clean low-to-high buckets.
  • The guitar reads like a rapid shimmer or tremolo layer.
  • The singer face can use more angular mouth and eyebrow shapes without losing the snowman charm.

Candy Cabaret

Peppermint show colors Playful front-yard energy Big expressions

This is the crowd-pleaser. It feels extra festive, theatrical, and a little over-the-top in exactly the fun way.

  • The striped bass and guitar can make string animation really obvious.
  • The singer face can go widest here without looking out of place.
  • The drummer can be the most animated performer, especially on crash and kick hits.

Shared sequencing spine: the standup bass uses four ordered lanes, the guitar uses six ordered lanes, the singer owns the strongest face logic, and the guitarist and bassist only light their face layers when background vocals are present.

Drummer carryover: crash arc plus sparkle decay, concentric kick pulse, hi-hat split and merge hit, snare flash disc, and six-frame arms that flail hardest on cymbal accents.

Suggested next step: if one concept jumps out, use it as the hero version and borrow one or two details from the others so you end up with a "best of" band instead of feeling forced to pick only one whole style.
